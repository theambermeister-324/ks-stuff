Discovery Questions

Tailored questions for Southwest stakeholders. Use these to deepen engagement and surface budget, timeline, and decision dynamics.

Workflow & Adoption

1
"One North found that Heart is treated as a suggestion. What would need to change for it to become a requirement?"
Surfaces real blockers — tooling, culture, leadership buy-in, or enforcement mechanisms
2
"When a designer starts a new project today, how do they know which Heart components to use — and which are production-ready?"
Exposes the documentation gap; validates "guidance without outcomes" finding
3
"How many developer pods are currently building components outside of Heart? What drives that decision?"
Quantifies siloed development — is it tooling friction or governance?

AI & Future State

4
"Which AI coding tools are your developers using today? Cursor, Copilot, Claude — or are teams choosing their own?"
Establishes the AI landscape; fragmentation reinforces MCP's "any AI tool" positioning
5
"When AI generates a UI component today, how does anyone verify it follows Heart's guidelines?"
Almost certainly "they don't" or "manual code review" — both lead directly to IPE
6
"One North's Phase 3 calls for MCP integration 12–18 months out. What would it mean if that capability was available in Phase 1?"
Plants the timeline compression narrative; gauge reaction to acceleration

Budget & Decision

7
"Is the budget for Heart improvements coming from the design system team, engineering, or a shared digital transformation fund?"
Identifies the economic buyer and whether this is design-led or engineering-led
8
"One North has scoped follow-on work. Is that budgeted? Would a platform that accelerates their roadmap change the investment calculus?"
Explores budget overlap; positions Knapsack as complementary, not competitive
9
"Who needs to sign off on a platform decision like this — and what does their evaluation process look like?"
Maps the buying committee and identifies procurement hurdles

Sequencing Tip

Lead with questions 1–3 in the first meeting. Save 7–9 for when you have a champion identified. Questions 4–6 are perfect for a demo or technical deep-dive session.

Objection Handling

Southwest-specific objections with reframes. Reference the One North diagnostic in every conversation.

"We're already working with One North on this. Why do we need Knapsack too?"

"One North diagnosed the problem and proposed the roadmap. Knapsack is the platform that operationalizes it." One North's expertise is in process design, workshops, and implementation planning. Knapsack provides the infrastructure — the living system of record, the MCP delivery layer, the metrics dashboard. One North even recommended MCP integration in Phase 3. We're the platform they'd point you toward.

Reframe: One North is the architect. Knapsack is the building. Both are needed.
"Heart isn't mature enough yet for an AI control plane. We need to stabilize first."

"That's exactly right — and IPE helps you stabilize faster." PCRI assessment gives you a quantified baseline on day one. Component Management provides maturity tiers and readiness statuses that One North's Phase 1 calls for. The metrics dashboard tracks stabilization progress in real-time. You don't need a mature system to start — you need a platform to get there efficiently.

Reframe: IPE is the scaffolding for maturity, not a reward for achieving it
"Our developers aren't using AI coding tools yet."

"Some of them are — they're just not telling you." 78% of enterprise developers use AI tools daily. The question isn't whether your devs will use AI — it's whether that AI will know about Heart. Getting governance in place before widespread adoption means you set the standard instead of chasing compliance.

Reframe: Governance before chaos, not governance after damage
"We can't justify another platform investment right now."

"You're already paying for the problem — you're just not seeing the invoice." Every time a developer builds a component outside Heart, every PR review that catches off-brand AI output, every designer who recreates a pattern from scratch — that's the cost. IPE Tier 0 is included for every Knapsack customer — start with zero incremental cost.

Reframe: Tier 0 is free. The cost of doing nothing is not.
"We need to see this work with our Storybook setup before committing."

"Perfect — Storybook is a first-class IPE source." IPE ingests Knapsack, Storybook, and Markdown at launch. Your existing Storybook investment becomes more valuable, not obsolete. A pilot can demonstrate multi-source ingestion with your actual Heart components in 2–4 weeks.

Reframe: We don't replace Storybook — we make it smarter
"How do we know adoption won't be the same problem — another tool people ignore?"

"That's the difference between a documentation tool and a control plane." Heart's adoption problem is that it's optional and hard to use. IPE makes Heart's context flow automatically to the AI tools devs already use — Cursor, Copilot, Claude. Devs don't adopt Knapsack; they adopt their AI tools. Knapsack makes those tools organization-aware behind the scenes.

Reframe: Adoption happens through tools they're already in
